About this model

This tool is a simplified demonstration model. It does not claim to measure a real person with precision. Its purpose is to show how cognitive profiles can differ as part of natural human variation, and how systems such as IQ-like evaluation or social/institutional interpretation can produce a distorted picture of the underlying profile.

Real profile

The real profile represents the underlying distribution of cognitive capacities. In this model, total points determine the overall average level, while variance determines how far the highest peaks and lowest troughs can be apart. This means that two profiles can have the same total cognitive capacity, but a very different internal shape.

Presets

Presets do not define intelligence level by themselves. They do not set how high the peaks are or how low the troughs are. That is determined by total points and variance.

Instead, presets influence where peaks and troughs are more likely to appear. Some domains are nudged upward, others downward, while the remaining domains are left free to vary naturally. In this way, the presets describe a profile shape, not a fixed type of person.

Why averages can mislead

A single average can hide important structure. A profile with strong peaks and deep troughs can have the same average as a much flatter profile, even though the lived reality may be very different. This is one of the central ideas of the model: a person can be misunderstood when visible output is treated as a reliable summary of their whole cognitive structure.

IQ-like inference

The IQ view in this tool does not try to reproduce psychometrics perfectly. Instead, it models institutional cognitive readability: the kinds of signals that systems often interpret as intelligence. These typically include task focus, symbolic reasoning, language output, executive control, and error monitoring.

In practice, this means that some profiles may be read as more intelligent than they really are overall, while others may be underestimated because their strongest capacities are less visible in formal task settings.

SCC: Societal Connectivity Capacities

SCC stands for Societal Connectivity Capacities. These are the capacities that influence how easily a person can connect with social systems that provide stability, safety, legitimacy, and support: family, school, work, institutions, care structures, and wider society.

SCC includes things like emotional readability, social signaling, self-awareness, communication, predictability, and the ability to be understood and taken seriously by others. A person can have deep intelligence and rich inner structure while still struggling to connect to systems that depend on recognizability and social legibility.

Status and social interpretation

Status is woven into both IQ-like and SCC-like interpretation. Some domains do not only affect how capable a person seems, but also how trustworthy, competent, relatable, or socially acceptable they appear.

This matters because people and institutions often do not react to the whole profile. They react to what stands out: confusion, friction, awkwardness, inconsistency, weak signaling, poor timing, low verbal clarity, or signs that someone seems less competent than expected. In other words, negative or unusual signals can weigh more heavily than hidden strengths.

What this tool is meant to show

This model is meant to show that human variation is not always well captured by simple scores, smooth averages, or narrow institutional lenses. Some profiles are easy for systems to read. Others are not. That difference can affect how a person is judged, supported, trusted, included, or overlooked.
